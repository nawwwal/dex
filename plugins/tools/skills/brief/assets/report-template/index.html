1 History Lesson

The Old Problem Shows You the Shape of This One

You are trying to explain a topic that looks new because the tools are new. The useful move is to find the older version of the same problem, then borrow the part that still works.

The section should not become trivia. The precedent earns its place only when it gives the reader a mechanism they can use right now.

2 How Another Field Solves It

Borrow the Rule Before You Borrow the Surface

Another field may already have a compact operating rule for the same kind of ambiguity. Use the rule, not the costume.

This is where the brief turns analogy into action: what should the reader change, sequence, remove, protect, or check?

3 A Way to Think

The Right Frame Separates Cheap Choices from Expensive Ones

A good frame reduces argument. It tells the reader what can be decided quickly and what deserves slower judgment.

Use this section to sort the topic, not summarize it. The reader should leave with a clearer order of operations.
