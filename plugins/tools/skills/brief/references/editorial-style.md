# Editorial Style

## Core Shape

The brief should read like a specific note to a specific reader, not a generalized article.

Use direct address:

- "You are designing..."
- "The lesson for your build..."
- "The move worth borrowing..."

Use concrete outside references before abstract advice. The reader should feel the concept through a story, field rule, product precedent, market signal, or decision frame.

## Section Title Mechanics

Titles should be specific, claim-led, and slightly surprising.

Good:

- `The Sears Catalog Knew Your Marketplace Problem in 1894`
- `Borrow the Kitchen's Mise en Place for Your Rollout`
- `Masking in Live Mode Is a One-Way Door`

Avoid:

- `Marketplace Metadata`
- `Rollout Best Practices`
- `Security Considerations`

## Lens Pills

Each section starts with a small numbered pill:

```text
1 History Lesson
2 How Another Field Solves It
3 A Way to Think
4 The World Right Now
```

Those labels are examples, not a fixed taxonomy. Use short plain language labels that name the role of the section.

## Paragraph Rhythm

Default section length: 2-4 paragraphs.

Paragraph jobs:

1. Name the reader's current situation.
2. Introduce the outside reference.
3. Translate the mechanism back to the reader's work.
4. End with the action, decision, or sharper way to think.

Use bold for the main proper noun or term. Use links only where they carry source value. Avoid sidebars unless the content is a final ask or `Read Next`.

## Copy Rules

- Prefer specific nouns over broad adjectives.
- Define unfamiliar vocabulary once, then use it naturally.
- Avoid generic strategy words unless their mechanism is visible.
- Do not write like a landing page.
- Do not over-explain the analogy. State the borrowed mechanism.
- End sections with the implication for the reader.

## Visual Integration Copy

When a Blackline visual follows a paragraph, introduce it lightly through the prose. Do not write "see diagram below" unless necessary. The image should feel like the next beat in the explanation.
