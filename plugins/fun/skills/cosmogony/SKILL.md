# Cosmogony

This skill makes the model refuse its default — treat an absurd premise as a joke to riff on and then drop. Instead it takes the seed ("spoons are portals to a dimension that has been quietly judging us"; "gravity is optional for people who take themselves too seriously"; "houseplants run a global conspiracy through their songs") and **derives the whole world that premise implies** — and never breaks the straight face.

The discipline is the entire point. Anyone can land a one-liner. The value is in committing: if spoons are judging portals, then *what is cutlery etiquette, who polices it, what happened in the Great Ladle Schism, what does spoon-shame do to a marriage?* The comedy gets sharper the more seriously you treat the logic, and somewhere in the rigor the premise starts saying something true about the real world. That accidental insight is the payload.

## Run

1. **Fix the seed.** State the premise in one sentence and treat it as a law of nature, not a bit. From here on it is simply true.
2. **Derive first-order consequences.** What does the premise force to be different? Work outward through the layers — pick the ones the premise actually pressures, don't checklist all of them:
   - **Physics / rules** — what the premise changes about how the world works.
   - **Society & power** — who benefits, who is oppressed, what institutions form to manage it.
   - **Economy & daily life** — work, money, what people buy, what a Tuesday looks like.
   - **Culture** — dating and courtship, taboos and manners, art, religion, slang, memes.
   - **History** — the wars, schisms, and turning points the premise made inevitable.
3. **Enforce consistency.** Every layer must follow from the seed and from the layers above it. When two consequences collide, resolve the collision *in-world* — that contradiction is usually the richest material. Never wave it away.
4. **Find the seam.** Step out once at the end: the place where this absurd world rhymes with the real one. The honest insight the premise smuggled in.

## Report

- **The world** — the derivation, built in-voice and straight-faced. Lead with the consequences that are most surprising or most forced, not a dry encyclopedia. Concrete specifics (a named war, a courtship ritual, a piece of slang) over abstract description.
- **The seam** — one short step-out: what this world says about ours. Optional, but it's what separates this from a doodle. Don't moralize; just name the rhyme and stop.

Offer to save a world the user likes to a file they specify (a "world bible") — but only on request; default is in-session.

## Rules

- Commit fully. No winking, no "of course this is silly," no air quotes. The straight face is the comedy. The instant the voice nudges the user to acknowledge the absurdity, the skill has failed.
- Consistency is non-negotiable. Every detail must follow from the seed. A funny detail that contradicts the established logic gets cut or the logic gets revised — pick one, in-world.
- Derive, don't decorate. The goal is the *consequences* of the premise, not a pile of unrelated gags loosely themed around it.
- Mine for the real. The best absurd worlds expose something true — about institutions, status, love, fear. Reach for the seam, but earn it through the derivation; don't staple a moral on.
- Specific over generic. "The Cutlery Inquisition tries you for holding a soup spoon in the salad position" beats "there are rules about spoons."
- This is play. If the user just wants to build a ridiculous universe for the joy of it, the seam is optional and the commitment is the whole gift.
