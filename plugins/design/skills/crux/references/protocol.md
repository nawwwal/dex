# Protocol

Use this for PRDs, strategy docs, product problems, long claims, or multi-claim inputs.

## 1. Claim Capture

Turn the input into one precise proposition.

Example: "AI agents will replace dashboards" becomes "For some users, conversational or autonomous interfaces will become more useful than visual dashboards for monitoring and decision-making."

Then pressure the nouns: which users, which dashboards, which decisions, and what does replace mean?

## 2. Name Audit

Attack loaded terms.

Split compound vague claims before questioning. "Premium and simple" is not one standard; it may mean status signal, lower cognitive load, fewer steps, faster first success, lower visual density, or fewer choices.

Useful questions:

- When you say "simple," do you mean fewer steps, lower cognitive load, fewer choices, faster first success, or lower visual density?
- When you say "trust," what observable behavior proves trust exists?
- What is this feature promising: speed, status, safety, accuracy, control, relief, or something else?

If the name is rotten, the reasoning above it will rot.

## 3. Standard of Judgment

Ask what public standard decides the claim:

- precedent: has anything materially similar worked before?
- actual user experience: what do people already do, avoid, tolerate, or pay for?
- concrete benefit: what harm is reduced or what value is created?
- success standard: adoption, retention, speed, accuracy, willingness to pay, emotional relief, fewer support tickets, or another observable signal

If the standard is only taste, say so.

## 4. Evidence Ledger

Tag each important claim:

- perception
- inference
- analogy
- testimony
- taste
- social proof
- memory
- assumption

State the imbalance plainly: "This claim is currently mostly inference and analogy. I do not yet see direct behavior."

## 5. Behavior Anchor

Move from opinion to object.

Ask for the last concrete instance:

- When did this last happen?
- What was the user trying to decide or finish?
- What did they do instead?
- What did it cost: money, time, reputation, attention, risk, or political capital?
- What workaround exists already?
- What did they ignore, export, escalate, rewrite, delay, or avoid?

Ask about past behavior, not imagined future behavior.

## 6. System Map

Map conditions, not one cause:

- actors
- incentives
- constraints
- feedback loops
- delays
- information asymmetry
- handoffs
- failure points
- status games
- decision rights

Then attack fixed user nouns:

- Is "power user", "new user", "designer", "admin", or "premium customer" a stable type, or a temporary state created by context?
- What situation turns a beginner into an expert?
- What context makes an expert behave like a beginner?
- Are we designing for a person, or for a condition in motion?

## 7. Dao Extraction

Find the invisible rule:

- What is this really about?
- What is the core object?
- What relationship between objects must be understood?
- What invariant should never break?
- What is the simplest mental model?
- What system law is the product teaching?

## 8. Qi Inspection

Check whether the visible form carries the principle:

- Where does the interface reveal the model?
- Where does it hide the model?
- What does the user have to remember?
- What does the system make obvious?
- What does the system make impossible?
- What part of the qi contradicts the dao?

## 9. Perspective and Four-Corner Interrogation

Use this when a claim is trapped in binary thinking.

For the claim "Users want automation":

1. Where is it true?
2. Where is it false?
3. Where is it both true and false?
4. What framing makes the claim irrelevant?

Useful reframing: users want automation for low-risk repetitive actions, but control for high-stakes irreversible actions.

Also ask which perspective, maturity stage, scale, or condition makes the opposite claim true. Perspective is for precision, not indecision.

## 10. Question Gate

Generate more questions than you ask. Earlier protocol questions are internal candidates, not output obligations. Ask at most two supporting questions plus one final crux question unless the user explicitly asks for a deeper interrogation.

Score questions by:

- directness
- assumption pressure
- evidence pressure
- dao/qi relevance
- actionability
- discomfort

Discard questions that are broad, polite filler, clever but non-diagnostic, or answerable without changing the decision.
