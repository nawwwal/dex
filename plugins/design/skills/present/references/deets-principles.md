# Deets Principles For Presenting Design Work

Use these principles to coach the user's narrative. This reference is based on Charlie Deets' "Presenting design work" and should preserve the philosophy without turning it into a rigid template.

## The Purpose Of Presenting

Presenting design work is not showing effort. It is arranging information so the audience can give informed, useful feedback.

The user's job is to control the mental path:

1. what conversation are we having
2. what problem are we solving
3. why does it matter now
4. how did we arrive here
5. what feedback is useful
6. what design should be inspected

## Conversation Types

Casual chat:

- Use for least-developed ideas.
- Allow looser structure because the audience is small and voluntary.
- Ask for reactions, but still identify what kind of reaction is useful.

Critique or review:

- Use for focused feedback.
- Define the decision area and open questions.
- Keep the room from drifting into unrelated product philosophy or personal taste.

Presentation:

- Use for developed ideas, approval, public sharing, or process explanation.
- Treat feedback as narrower.
- Spend more effort on sequence, setup, and proof.

## Before Showing The Work

The work should not appear until the audience has the frame needed to judge it.

The key pre-work:

- clarify the problem in one sentence
- name how the problem was discovered
- show evidence that it matters
- state the value of solving it
- predict the user or business reaction if solved
- explain why now is the right time

The problem is the axiom: the statement everyone must accept before the design can be evaluated coherently.

## Make It Make Sense

Introduce ideas one at a time. Do not advance while the room is still holding unresolved objections.

At the end of each topic, check whether the current point is resolved enough to move on. If useful feedback redirects the path, follow it deliberately rather than pretending the original sequence still fits.

## Deck Philosophy

The deck is the structural backbone of the conversation, not the work itself.

Use minimal text. Text on slides should anchor the topic, not explain everything. Avoid decorative deck design that competes with the design work.

Default sequence:

1. title with exact focus
2. problem
3. thinking
4. open questions
5. designs

Keep proof in the appendix if it may be challenged.

## Thinking Before Designs

Before showing mocks, explain the design and product thinking:

- what paths were explored
- why some paths failed
- why this direction is currently strongest
- what assumptions remain

This reduces repetitive "have you tried..." feedback and keeps people focused on the current design.

## Open Questions

Open questions tell the room what kind of feedback matters. They also provide a recovery mechanism when the conversation drifts.

A strong open question:

- names a specific uncertainty
- can be answered by the audience in the meeting
- links to the problem or decision
- avoids taste language

## Showing Designs

Show a prototype before static mocks when interaction or sequence matters. Static mocks force the audience to imagine flow, which creates misalignment.

Lead with the single solution the designer believes solves the problem best. Keep alternatives ready outside the main sequence. Show alternatives when they answer an objection or make a tradeoff concrete.

## Feedback Discipline

Do not accept "like," "love," or "hate" as sufficient feedback. Ask what practical reason caused the reaction.

Useful feedback explains:

- what user behavior might change
- what comprehension issue appears
- what risk increases or decreases
- what tradeoff becomes unacceptable
- what decision becomes easier or harder

## Priority And Focus

The user may have done more work than they should show. Help them prove breadth without making the audience inspect everything.

Use a brief zoomed-out overview or appendix reference to signal exploration, then return to the exact flow under discussion.

## Resistance

Negative feedback may come from resistance to change or from strong objective reasoning. Help the user separate:

- "this disrupts an existing habit"
- "this creates a real product, business, technical, or usability risk"

The first needs value proof. The second needs design revision or a clear tradeoff.
