# Narrative Patterns

Use these patterns when the user asks for a deck outline, review script, presentation arc, or rehearsal structure.

## Default Review Spine

1. Focus: exact title that names the decision area.
2. Problem: one sentence everyone must agree with.
3. Evidence: why the problem is real and worth solving now.
4. Goal: what should change if the work succeeds.
5. Thinking: constraints, explored paths, and why this direction is strongest.
6. Open questions: what feedback is useful today.
7. Primary flow: prototype first if interaction matters.
8. Decision points: where judgment is needed.
9. Alternatives: only as response to objections or tradeoffs.
10. Close: decisions made, feedback captured, next step.

## Prep Spine

Use when the user wants to be ready for the room, not just ready with slides.

1. Meeting purpose: what the conversation is really for.
2. Decision boundary: what is open, closed, and out of scope.
3. Belief sequence: what the room must understand before seeing the design.
4. Vulnerable claim: the weakest important claim in the narrative.
5. Likely objections: who will resist, and on what practical basis.
6. Sharp questions: what the user should ask to uncover hidden assumptions.
7. Recovery moves: how to redirect taste feedback, scope drift, or premature alternatives.
8. Closing ask: the exact decision, feedback, or next input needed.

## Approval Meeting

Use when the work is mature and the audience needs confidence.

Emphasize:

- decision needed
- evidence
- risk reduction
- why alternatives are weaker
- what will happen after approval

Avoid:

- broad ideation
- showing many options as if the direction is undecided
- asking taste questions

Useful opener:

"I want to align on whether this direction solves [problem] well enough to move into [next step]. The main feedback I need is around [risk/tradeoff], not [closed topic]."

## Critique Or Design Review

Use when the work needs sharper feedback.

Emphasize:

- stage of fidelity
- open questions
- constraints
- what is intentionally unresolved
- which parts should not be judged yet

Avoid:

- pretending rough work is final
- defending every decision
- asking the room to critique everything

Useful opener:

"This is at [stage]. I am not looking for feedback on [closed area] yet. I need help deciding [question] because it affects [next design move]."

## Stakeholder Alignment

Use when the design is a vehicle for cross-functional agreement.

Emphasize:

- shared problem
- stakeholder-specific concern
- tradeoff
- decision boundary
- operational consequence

Avoid:

- design craft language as the main argument
- hidden assumptions
- letting one stakeholder's concern redefine the whole problem without naming it

Useful opener:

"The design is here to make one tradeoff visible: [tradeoff]. If we agree on that, the flow becomes easier to judge."

## Concept Exploration

Use when the work is early but needs a useful conversation.

Emphasize:

- hypothesis
- behavior change
- what the concept proves or disproves
- next learning step

Avoid:

- overclaiming certainty
- asking for approval
- polishing the deck more than clarifying the hypothesis

Useful opener:

"This is not a final direction. I am using it to test whether [behavior/hypothesis] is worth pursuing."

## Public Process Share

Use when the user is explaining completed work, learning, or process.

Emphasize:

- original problem
- discovery
- constraints
- turning points
- final decision logic
- what others can learn

Avoid:

- linear success story if the real value came from tradeoffs
- showing every artifact
- making the conclusion sound inevitable

Useful opener:

"The useful part of this work is not just the final screen. It is how the problem changed once we understood [learning]."
