# Mom Test Principles for User Panel Briefs

**Source:** "The Mom Test" by Rob Fitzpatrick
**Context:** Applied to Razorpay user panel research brief quality

---

## Core Principle

**The Mom Test:** Talk about their life instead of your idea. Ask about specifics in the past instead of generics or opinions about the future.

---

## Rule 1: Talk About Their Life, Not Your Idea

### ❌ Bad Questions (Feature-Centered)
- "Do you like the new reports dashboard?"
- "Would you use this feature?"
- "Is this design clear?"

### ✅ Good Questions (User-Centered)
- "How do you currently generate monthly reports?"
- "Walk me through the last time you needed financial data."
- "What workarounds have you built for reporting?"

**Why:** Talking about your idea triggers politeness bias. People lie to avoid hurting your feelings.

---

## Rule 2: Ask About Specifics in the Past

### ❌ Bad Questions (Future Intent)
- "Would you be interested in automated reporting?"
- "How often would you use this feature?"
- "Do you think you'd pay for this?"

### ✅ Good Questions (Past Behavior)
- "How many times in the last 30 days did you download a report?"
- "When was the last time you manually reconciled payments?"
- "How much time did that take?"

**Why:** Hypotheticals are fiction. Past behavior is data.

---

## Rule 3: Listen More Than You Talk

### ❌ Leading Questions
- "Our new design makes it easier to find reports. Don't you think that's helpful?"
- "Most users struggle with this. Do you struggle too?"

### ✅ Open-Ended Questions
- "How do you currently find the report you need?"
- "What's the hardest part of your current process?"

**Why:** If you're pitching, you're not learning.

---

## Applying Mom Test to Screener Questions

### Purpose of Screeners
Filter for users who have **experienced the problem** (not users who might be interested).

### ❌ Bad Screeners (Hypothetical)
**Q:** "Would you be interested in using a user panel for research?"
**Problem:** Everyone says yes to be helpful. Doesn't filter.

### ✅ Good Screeners (Behavioral)
**Q:** "How many times in the last 60 days did you conduct user research?"
**Desired Answer:** "3+ times"
**Why:** Filters for active researchers who actually do this work.

## Common Pitfalls in Research Briefs

### Pitfall 1: Asking for Opinions Instead of Facts

❌ **Bad:**
- "Do you think our onboarding is too long?"
- "Would you prefer email or WhatsApp notifications?"

✅ **Good:**
- "How long did your onboarding take? Was that acceptable?"
- "How did you receive your last notification from us? What did you do with it?"

---

### Pitfall 2: Asking Yes/No Questions

❌ **Bad:**
- "Do you use reports often?"
- "Is this feature useful?"

✅ **Good:**
- "How often do you use reports? Walk me through your routine."
- "What do you use this feature for? Give me an example."

**Why:** Yes/no questions don't reveal context or depth.

---

### Pitfall 3: Solution-Validation Instead of Problem-Discovery

❌ **Bad Brief Focus:**
"We built a new reports dashboard. We want to test if users find it intuitive."

✅ **Good Brief Focus:**
"Users abandon the reports page. We want to understand their current reporting workflow and pain points."

**Why:** If you're testing your solution, you've skipped discovery. You might be solving the wrong problem.

---

## Checklist: Is My Brief Mom Test-Compliant?

### Problem Statement
- [ ] Describes user problem (not solution)
- [ ] Specific and observable
- [ ] Grounded in real user behavior (not assumptions)

### Research Questions
- [ ] All questions open-ended ("How", "Why", "What")
- [ ] Ask about past behavior (not future intent)
- [ ] Non-leading (don't suggest an answer)
- [ ] No yes/no questions

### Screener Questions
- [ ] Filter for past behavior ("How often in the last X days...")
- [ ] Specific enough to find right users
- [ ] No hypothetical scenarios ("Would you be interested...")

### Methodology Alignment
- [ ] Method matches goal (discovery → interview, usability → test)
- [ ] Questions will actually answer the hypothesis
- [ ] No missing questions for stated research goals

## Examples from Razorpay Context

### Example 1: Reports Dashboard Research

❌ **Bad Brief:**
- Problem: "We want to test the new reports dashboard design"
- Question: "Do you like the new layout?"
- Screener: "Would you use a reports feature?"

✅ **Good Brief:**
- Problem: "Users abandon reports page within 30 seconds. We believe it's due to unclear navigation."
- Question: "Walk me through the last time you needed a financial report. What did you do?"
- Screener: "How many times in the last 30 days did you download a financial report?" (Answer: "5+ times")

---

### Example 2: Payment Onboarding Research

❌ **Bad Brief:**
- Problem: "We redesigned onboarding to be faster"
- Question: "Do you think 5 steps is better than 10?"
- Screener: "Are you a business owner?"

✅ **Good Brief:**
- Problem: "Users abandon payment onboarding at Step 3 (bank details). We hypothesize they don't trust us with sensitive data."
- Question: "Think back to when you first set up payments. At what point did you hesitate? What made you continue or stop?"
- Screener: "In the last 6 months, have you set up a new payment gateway for your business?" (Answer: "Yes, within last 6 months")
